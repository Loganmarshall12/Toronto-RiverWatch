What is Toronto RiverWatch?

A map-based flood risk visualization tool for Toronto that aggregates environmental data to compare relative flood risk across neighbourhoods.

"To create a Toronto where residents and visitors are empowered with clear, accessible information about flood risk, enabling informed decisions that help protect lives, property, and communities."

Project Vision Statement

The Whys and Hows

Flood risk data exists across multiple sources but is difficult to interpret or compare at a neighbourhood scale. Changes in environmental factors can increase flood risk over time — RiverWatch brings it all together.

What it does

Visualizes monthly flood risk using choropleth maps. Integrates precipitation and river gauge data, overlays regulated floodplain boundaries, and allows month-by-month scenario comparison.

How it works

Environmental data is collected — precipitation, river flow, floodplain boundaries. Neighbourhoods are linked to their nearest monitoring stations and a risk score is calculated combining these factors.

Data sources

Toronto Open Data, Toronto and Region Conservation Authority (TRCA), and Environment Canada.

Methodology

Each neighbourhood receives a risk score between 0.0 and 1.0, calculated monthly from real monitored data. In Sprint 4 we added interactive “What‑If” scenario sliders that let you explore how changes in policy, land use, or climate could affect flood risk across Toronto.

Base risk formula

Each factor is weighted by its relative importance to flood risk:

Risk = Base + (Flow × 0.30) + (Rain × 0.20) + Floodplain (0.20)
  • Base risk – Baseline value assigned per neighbourhood
  • River flow (30%) – Weighted effect from nearby river gauge data
  • Precipitation (20%) – Weighted effect from monthly rainfall totals
  • Floodplain bonus (20%) – Added if neighbourhood intersects a regulated floodplain

Final score is capped between 0.0 and 1.0. Missing station data falls back to neighbourhood or city‑wide averages.

What‑If scenario sliders

On the main map, use the three sliders to simulate changes:

  • Policy Multiplier – Models stricter (↓) or laxer (↑) floodplain regulations. Range 0.5–1.5.
  • Greenery / Land‑Use – Simulates more green infrastructure (↓ risk) or more pavement (↑ risk). Range 0.5–1.5.
  • Climate Multiplier – Represents wetter (↑) or drier (↓) future climate patterns. Range 0.5–1.5.

Adjusted risk is calculated as:

AdjustedRisk = clamp(OriginalRisk × Policy × LandUse × Climate, 0, 1)

Click Apply Scenario to see the map update instantly. Reset returns to real measured risk.

Risk categories

High Risk (0.7 – 1.0) Medium Risk (0.4 – 0.7) Low Risk (0.0 – 0.4)

These colours update dynamically when you apply a scenario, helping you visualise how policy or climate changes could shift flood risk across Toronto neighbourhoods.

100 Year Storm Scenario

This scenario simulates a major flood event based on Environment and Climate Change Canada (ECCC) Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) statistics.

Figure 1: ECCC IDF statistics showing the 97.3mm 24-hour rainfall threshold for Toronto (Update 2021).

The Hydrology Logic

Our calculation models how a 100-year storm (approx. 97.3mm of rainfall over 24 hours) interacts with Toronto's unique urban landscape. We use a weighted multi-factor formula to determine localized risk:

Risk = Base + (Flow * 3.5 * 0.30) + (Rain * 1.6 * 0.20) + 0.25*
  • Precipitation (1.6× Multiplier): Toronto’s median 24-hour event is ~60mm. The 100-year storm of 97mm represents a 1.6× increase over baseline rainfall totals.
  • River Flow (3.5× Multiplier): Due to high impervious surface coverage (pavement), runoff is amplified. We apply a 3.5× multiplier to gauge data to reflect efficient water transport into the river systems.
  • Floodplain Bonus (+0.25): A flat risk addition is applied to any neighborhood intersecting a regulated floodplain, as these areas face inherent structural risk regardless of current rainfall.

Source: ECCC Engineering Cityfloodmap Climate Datasets & IDF Statistics (climate.weather.gc.ca).
